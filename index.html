Anti-Wave Acoustic Focusing

Classical standing waves in a cavity have zero net energy flow. This model introduces an "Anti-Wave" phase gradient, an active control mechanism that directs acoustic energy. This transforms the chamber from a passive resonator into an active focusing device.

The result is a non-zero, time-averaged energy flux, concentrating mechanical stress onto specific targets like the granite sarcophagus.

Governing Equation:

I(r) = (1 / 2ρω) ∇P²(r) · sin(2Δφ(r))

Where a non-zero phase gradient Δφ(r) guarantees a directed energy flux I(r).

Hydro-Acoustic Amplification

A key challenge is transferring energy from air into solid granite, as their acoustic impedances are vastly different, causing most energy to reflect. The model proposes a sub-chamber water column (Well Shaft) to solve this.

Water acts as an intermediary, efficiently coupling the vibrations to the granite. This concept of using a massive, phase-coherent fluid driver parallels principles from Nikola Tesla's Wardenclyffe experiments for large-scale energy transfer.

Impedance Matching:

  • Air: ~415 Rayl
  • Water: ~1,480,000 Rayl
  • Granite: ~16,200,000 Rayl

The transmission coefficient from air to water is nearly perfect (T ≈ 0.999), demonstrating its effectiveness as an energy coupler.

Piezoelectric Transduction

The final step is converting focused mechanical stress into electricity. The Red Aswan Granite used in the chambers is rich in quartz, a piezoelectric crystal. This means it generates a voltage when compressed.

When the focused acoustic wave applies oscillating pressure (stress) to the granite, the quartz crystals within generate an oscillating electric field—the system's primary electromagnetic output.

Induced Electric Field:

|E_induced| ≈ (d_eff / ε_granite) · σ_max

The output voltage is directly proportional to the applied mechanical stress (σ_max).

Nonlinear Harmonics

Under the immense pressure created by focused resonance, the granite structure no longer behaves linearly. This high-stress environment causes a phenomenon known as Second Harmonic Generation.

The system effectively "up-converts" the low input frequency into a higher frequency. This is critical, as higher frequencies propagate more efficiently through conductive materials, allowing the generated energy to be transmitted.
